Part One

What Kind of Film Is This?

Before anything else: adjust your expectations. The Grandmaster is not an action film, though it contains extraordinary action. It is not a biography, though it centres on a real man. It is a meditation — on time, on loss, on the cost of preserving beauty in a world that keeps destroying it.

Director Wong Kar-wai is known for films that feel more like memories than stories: fragmented, gorgeous, emotionally precise even when narratively elusive. He spent a decade researching this film, immersing himself in martial arts lineages and Republican-era Chinese history. His camera lingers on faces, fabric, rain, falling snow — the visual world of the film is as important as any dialogue.

You will encounter slow motion used not for spectacle but for feeling: the way a moment stretches when it matters. You will hear overlapping narration from characters reflecting on their lives from a distance. The film moves back and forward in time — Wong Kar-wai is interested in how the past haunts the present, and in how much of what we love is always already lost.

A note on versions

The film exists in several cuts. The full Hong Kong version (130 minutes) is richer in historical detail and gives more time to secondary characters. A shorter international cut (108 minutes) streamlines the story. This guide is written to serve both, focusing on the cultural knowledge you need regardless of which version you watch.

Part Two

The World They Live In — Historical Context

The film spans roughly two decades, from the mid-1930s to the early 1950s. This was one of the most violent and transformative periods in modern Chinese history. To understand the characters' losses — and why they carry themselves with such gravitas — you need a sense of what they lived through.

1912 — The Fall of the Qing Dynasty

China's last imperial dynasty collapsed, ending over 2,000 years of imperial rule. A republic was declared, but the country quickly fractured into competing warlord territories. This political chaos inadvertently created a golden age of martial arts: without a strong central government to suppress them, independent schools of kung fu flourished across China, especially in the prosperous south.

1931–1937 — Japanese Invasion Begins

Japan seized Manchuria (northeast China) in 1931 and began pushing deeper into the country. By 1937, full-scale war erupted — the Second Sino-Japanese War. Cities fell, communities were destroyed, and millions of people were displaced. Martial arts masters, like everyone else, lost their homes, their livelihoods, and often their families.

1937–1945 — Occupation and Survival

Japanese forces occupied much of China. The film's characters navigate this period differently — some resist, some survive by compromise, some lose everything. You will see characters who were prosperous grandmasters reduced to poverty, teaching in cramped rooms to survive. This is not failure; it is history acting on human lives.

1945–1949 — Civil War and the Communist Victory

After Japan's defeat, China erupted into civil war between the Nationalist government (Kuomintang) and Communist forces led by Mao Zedong. In 1949, the Communists won. The Nationalists retreated to Taiwan. Millions fled to Hong Kong — including Ip Man, the film's central figure. For martial artists, this meant the complete destruction of the traditional world they had inhabited.

1950s — Exile in Hong Kong

The film's final act takes place here. Hong Kong, then a British colony, became a refuge city. Masters who had commanded respect across China now teach in cramped apartments to make ends meet. Everything they knew — their schools, their students, their social world — is gone. What remains is their art, and the question of whether it can survive them.

Why this matters

When characters in the film speak of having "lost everything" or of a "golden age" that is gone, they mean this literally. The world they grew up in — a China of independent masters, grand martial arts halls, and elaborate ceremonies of respect — was physically destroyed. The melancholy that runs through the film is not personal melodrama. It is historical grief.

Part Three

The Martial Arts World — Jianghu (江湖)

The single most important concept for understanding this film is jianghu — literally "rivers and lakes," but meaning something far richer: the parallel social world of martial artists, wanderers, and independent practitioners that exists alongside mainstream Chinese society, governed by its own unwritten codes of honour.

Think of jianghu as something between the American Wild West and a medieval guild system — a space where skill, reputation, and personal honour matter more than law or government authority. In this world, a martial arts master occupies a position of immense social prestige. Their school is not just a gym; it is a lineage, a family, a piece of living cultural heritage.

How jianghu society works

In jianghu, status is earned through martial skill and moral conduct — not wealth or birth. A master who defeats challengers with grace, who teaches with integrity, who keeps their word in all things, accumulates enormous reputation. That reputation is the currency of their world.

The jianghu has its own ceremonies. When a travelling martial artist enters a new city, they must first pay formal respects to the local senior master — failure to do so is a profound insult. When two masters meet for the first time, an elaborate ritual of bowing and greeting establishes their relative status. Challenges between schools are governed by strict rules about how they are issued, accepted, and conducted. Even how you lose a fight matters enormously.

Part Four

Key Cultural Concepts

Face — 面子 (miànzi)

Chinese culture makes a crucial distinction between two concepts that English collapses into a single word: face. 面子 (miànzi) is your social face — your reputation, your standing, how others perceive and respect you. It is earned through achievement and maintained through conduct. Lian (脸) is your moral face — your inner sense of dignity and the shame you would feel at wrongdoing.

In jianghu, mianzi is everything. Losing face is not embarrassment — it is social destruction. A master who loses face loses students, allies, and the right to operate in the martial arts world. This is why challenges in the film are conducted with such elaborate ceremony, and why characters will accept enormous personal sacrifice rather than take a shortcut that would cost them their reputation.

Watch for moments when characters deliberately give face to each other — acknowledging an opponent's skill, deferring in small ways, framing a defeat as honourable. These are acts of profound respect in this world.

The Hidden Lining — 里子 (lǐzi)

面子 (miànzi) has a counterpart: 里子 (lǐzi) — literally the lining of a garment, the layer hidden entirely from public view. If 面子 (miànzi) is the visible face of a person or institution, 里子 (lǐzi) is what operates beneath it: the unseen power, the private understanding, the things that never appear on the surface but hold everything together.

In jianghu, the most formidable figures are not always the most visible ones. A school's grandmaster may be its 面子 (miànzi) — the public face who receives challengers, delivers speeches, and carries the reputation of his lineage. Behind him there may be a 里子 (lǐzi): someone whose authority derives precisely from being unknown, whose value lies in what they can do that the public face cannot. The two roles are not in conflict; they are complementary, each enabling the other.

Watch for characters whose power comes from obscurity rather than reputation — and for the film's interest in the price each kind of power exacts from the person who holds it.

Master and Disciple — Shifu (師父) and Tudi (徒弟)

The relationship between a martial arts master and their student runs far deeper than teacher and pupil. The word for "master" — shifu — combines the characters for "teacher" and "father." This is not metaphorical. A disciple owes their master filial piety: obedience, care in old age, and the duty of passing on the lineage.

A student who betrays their master commits an act of moral horror comparable to patricide. Conversely, a master who fails in their duty to a student carries shame. The film's deepest tensions revolve around these obligations: what you owe your master, what you owe your art, and when the two conflict.

Passing on the Lineage

In traditional Chinese martial arts, a style's techniques were closely guarded secrets passed only to trusted disciples. A master embodied not just fighting knowledge but a living link to every teacher who came before. When a style died with a master — when they died without a worthy successor — it was understood as a kind of cultural death.

Part Five

North and South — The Great Divide

One of the film's central tensions is the geographical and philosophical divide between Northern and Southern Chinese martial arts. This is a real historical distinction, not a fictional one. The Yangtze River traditionally separated the two worlds, each with its own character, techniques, and cultural identity.

Northern styles

Northern China's vast plains and cold climate shaped its martial arts: long-range techniques, high kicks, sweeping leg movements, and powerful whole-body movements that use space and momentum. Northern styles emphasise what practitioners call "internal" power — cultivating energy (qi) through breathing and mental focus. The Baguazhang and Xingyiquan practised in the Gong family, and the Wudang tradition Gong Yutian represents, are all Northern arts.

Southern styles

Southern China's rivers, mountains, and dense geography produced tighter, lower techniques: close-range striking, stable footwork, rapid hand techniques. Wing Chun — Ip Man's art — is quintessentially Southern: economical, direct, built for close quarters. Southern styles have historically been described as "external" arts, focused on the refinement of physical technique.

The film's opening chapters unfold in Foshan — a city in Guangdong province that held near-mythological status in the Southern martial arts world. It was home to dozens of prominent schools; Wing Chun and several other major Southern styles took their modern form there. When the Japanese invasion forces its masters to flee, Foshan's fall registers in the film not as a single displacement but as the obliteration of the Southern tradition's living centre.

Part Six

Martial Arts Styles You Will Encounter

Wing Chun

詠春 · Yǒng Chūn

Ip Man's style — Southern

A close-range Southern style known for rapid, simultaneous attack and defence. Its most famous technique, the "wooden dummy," trains practitioners against a stationary target. Economical and precise — every movement has a function. Wing Chun was historically associated with the Shaolin Temple and was considered somewhat secretive; Ip Man's decision to teach it openly in Hong Kong was radical.

Baguazhang

八卦掌 · Bā Guà Zhǎng

Gong Er's style — Northern

Literally "Eight Trigrams Palm" — its name references the eight symbols of the I Ching (易经, yì jīng), the ancient Chinese text of divination and philosophy. Baguazhang is built around circular walking and evasion: practitioners spiral around opponents rather than meeting force with force. Gong Er's signature move, the "64 Hands," references Bagua's 64 transformations. Among them is 叶底藏花 (yè dǐ cáng huā — "flowers hidden beneath leaves"): a technique that embodies Baguazhang's governing principle — and the meaning of 出其不意 (chū qí bù yì), striking where the opponent least expects it. Beauty hidden under the surface; force revealed only at the moment it cannot be defended against. Watch how she moves — always in curves, never in straight lines.

Bajiquan

八極拳 · Bā Jí Quán

"The Razor's" style — Northern

Literally "Eight Extremities Fist" — an explosive Northern style known for sudden, devastating short-range power. Bajiquan was historically taught to imperial bodyguards (it protected both Mao Zedong and Chiang Kai-shek). Its techniques emphasise elbow strikes, shoulder checks, and shocking bursts of force. The character Yixiantian ("The Razor") uses this style — when you see him fight, notice the explosive, percussive quality of each strike.

Xingyiquan

形意拳 · Xíng Yì Quán

Gong family tradition — Northern

Literally "Form-Intent Fist" — one of the three great Northern arts alongside Baguazhang and Taijiquan. Where Baguazhang circles and evades, Xingyiquan advances in straight lines with explosive, controlled force. Its movements are drawn from five elemental forms and twelve animal archetypes — each encoding a distinct quality of power and intention. In the Gong family lineage, Xingyiquan and Baguazhang are not separate arts but complementary expressions of the same underlying philosophy: that fighting form and inner intention must become one.

How to watch the fights

The action choreographer is the legendary Yuen Woo-ping, who also choreographed Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon and The Matrix. But Wong Kar-wai uses these fights differently than action cinema normally does. Pay attention to the philosophy expressed in each style's movement: Gong Er's spiralling evasions embody her character's indirectness and patience; Ip Man's straight, economical strikes embody his directness and clarity. The fights are character portraits.

A Final Note

Before the First Frame

Wong Kar-wai has said that he wanted to examine "the whole martial arts landscape" of an era — not just one man's story, but the spirit of an entire civilisation at a moment of catastrophic transformation. The grandmasters in this film are not merely skilled fighters. They are custodians of a philosophy expressed through the body, a way of understanding the world that was nearly obliterated by the twentieth century.

The film's ultimate question is simple and vast: when a world ends, what survives? The answer it offers is quietly hopeful. Art survives. Discipline survives. The right attitude — toward your practice, toward your opponents, toward loss itself — survives in the people it has shaped. Ip Man carries Wing Chun to Hong Kong in his body and his bearing. A small room. A handful of students. One of them becomes Bruce Lee. The tradition persists.

Settle in. Watch slowly. Let the images do their work.
